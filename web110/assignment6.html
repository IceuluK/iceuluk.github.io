Activity: Practice web browser safety
• Review browser options
In my browser (Google Chrome), I clicked “More” menu (three dots) at the top right > Settings > Security and Privacy, and reviewed my settings:
- blocked third-party cookies
- “Do Not Track” request with my browsing traffic option turned on
- always use secure connections
- Standard protection against websites, downloads, extensions that are known to be dangerous (no browsing data sent to Google)
- warn if my passwords are exposed in a data breach
I reflected on my web browser safety and think it is acceptable but there’s room for improvement (I do store passwords saved in Google Chrome for convenience).

• Check your browser's version to ensure the browser is up to date.
To check browser’s version, I went to More > Settings > About Chrome, and made sure the browser was up to date.


• Check add-ons to ensure they are up to date.
In Google Chrome, I clicked More > More tools > Extensions, and clicked “Update” on the add-ons page, to bring them up to date.

• Clear the browser cache. Identify how to clear browser history and cookies.
To clear browser cache in Google Chrome, one needs to go to More menu > Settings > Security and Privacy > Clear browsing data > select time range > Cached images and files

• Check auto form settings and remove any personal information you do not want saved.
In Google Chrome, I went to More menu > Settings > Autofill, and reviewed the settings. I have a lot of passwords stored for autofill, as well as a few payment methods and addresses. I feel like it’s a battle between better security and convenience, and the latter often wins.

• Visit a secure website, such as https://en.wikiversity.org and view certificate information for that website.
To view a certificate on any website, one needs to click the lock icon next to the domain name > Connection is secure > Certificate is valid. It is a good practice to check for a website’s TLS/SSL certificate to make sure it uses secure, encrypted connection and is less vulnerable to cybercrimes like man-in-the middle attack etc.


• Use private / incognito browsing to visit a website that normally remembers you. Notice how you are unknown to the website when using private browsing.
In Google Chrome, I clicked More menu > New Incognito Window > Gmail. The browser wanted me to sign in in Gmail in incognito mode since it didn’t have the cookies and my saved information.

With this activity, I was able to review and reflect on my web browser privacy and security settings. At work, I am much more vigilant – there is no autofill of any forms and there are no saved passwords in my work laptop’s browser. This activity made me think of getting a paid VPN subscription for my personal laptop, as well as getting a password manager as 1Password.

Activity: Test your firewall using a testing service such as Gibson Research: ShieldsUP!
After reviewing the offered resource, I learned some new information:
Universal Plug n' Play (UPnP) is a “zero-authentication” (no passwords required) system for allowing devices to discover and connect with each other on a private local network. It used to be enabled by default in consumer Internet routers since 2002 or 2003. Any home appliance — TV's, game consoles, printers, fax machines, etc. includes support for UPnP.
UPnP was never meant to be exposed to the external public Internet but its exposure gives malicious actors direct access to the inside of an exposed private network.
